01 — The Scale

This isn't a rare failure. It's the rule.

Every year, roughly 600,000 people are released from U.S. prisons. Within five years, nearly three-quarters will be rearrested. We've been treating this as a moral problem. What if it's a structural one?

These numbers have remained stubbornly consistent for decades despite billions spent on incarceration. If the system were working as a deterrent, we'd expect to see improvement. We don't. That should prompt a question: what if the theory of change — that punishment deters — is built on a flawed premise about human agency?

02 — The Assumption

The story we tell ourselves

"They chose to reoffend." This is the implicit premise of retributive justice — the idea that punishment is deserved because the person freely chose to do wrong, and could have chosen otherwise.

It's a philosophically coherent position. Kant argued that to deny someone's moral responsibility is actually to deny their humanity — to treat them as a machine rather than a rational agent. There's something genuinely important in that intuition.

"To act freely is to act from reason, not from inclination or circumstance."

— IMMANUEL KANT, GROUNDWORK FOR THE METAPHYSICS OF MORALS (1785)

But this model makes an assumption: that at the moment of choice, circumstances are not the dominant force. That the person standing at a decision point is, in any meaningful sense, equally free to go either way.

The data has something uncomfortable to say about that assumption.

03 — The Predictors

What actually predicts reoffending

Using data from the NIJ Recidivism Challenge dataset — nearly 26,000 individuals tracked over three years — a logistic regression model trained only on pre-choice variables achieves strong predictive accuracy.

The key word: pre-choice. These are factors fixed before any "decision" to reoffend was ever made. No measure of intent, motivation, or character. Only circumstance.

04 — The Model

How predictable is "choice"?

Three models — logistic regression, random forest, and gradient boosting — were trained using only unchosen variables. All three converge on AUC-ROC around 0.70, using only information that existed before any decision was made.

Adding "partially chosen" variables (employment, education, housing) improves prediction by just 0.012 AUC. The unchosen variables carry 98% of the signal.

The critical insight isn't the raw AUC. It's the gap — or rather the lack of one — between the unchosen-only model and the full model. The "partially chosen" variables (things a person has some agency over, like staying in school or finding work) add almost nothing once you already know their neighborhood, family history, and age at first arrest.

05 — The Philosophy

Three answers to an uncomfortable question

Philosophers have argued for centuries about whether determinism and moral responsibility can coexist. The data gives this debate new teeth.

Kant — Hard Retributivism
"Punishment is deserved because the rational agent chose freely."
Kant's framework requires that punishment be proportional to the wrong freely chosen. Circumstances are irrelevant — the moral law is accessible to all rational beings regardless of situation.
VERDICT ON THE DATA → Kant must claim the 70% prediction rate is coincidence, not causation. The agent was still free in every instance. Hard to sustain.
Strawson — Reactive Attitudes
"Responsibility is about relationships and attitudes, not metaphysical freedom."
P.F. Strawson argued that holding someone responsible is a social practice — about resentment, gratitude, and moral engagement — not about whether the universe is deterministic.
VERDICT ON THE DATA → Strawson's view survives determinism but struggles with systematic disadvantage. Can we justifiably resent someone for what their circumstances made nearly inevitable?
Dennett — Compatibilism
"Freedom worth wanting is real even in a deterministic world."
Daniel Dennett's compatibilism holds that what matters is whether behavior responds to reasons — not whether it was "uncaused." A person who acts on deliberation is free enough to be responsible.
VERDICT ON THE DATA → Most plausible — but demands we ask: if circumstances systematically undermine reason-responsiveness, at what point does responsibility transfer to the system that creates those circumstances?

The most honest answer sits between these views: people are neither fully free nor fully determined. But the data suggests we've been distributing moral responsibility far too heavily onto individuals and far too lightly onto the structural conditions that constrain them.

06 — The Policy Consequence

What changes if we're right?

If behavior is substantially shaped by unchosen circumstances, then a justice system built primarily on punishment and deterrence is targeting the wrong variable. The natural experiment: countries that treat crime as a structural problem.

The typical objection: Norway is smaller, more homogeneous, wealthier. True. But the gap is so dramatic — 68% vs. 20% — that cultural differences can't account for all of it. And the underlying logic tracks with what the data shows: address the structural conditions that predict reoffending, and reoffending decreases.

The pattern is consistent: interventions that treat recidivism as a personal failing (longer sentences, harsher conditions) show weak evidence. Interventions that address the structural predictors the model identifies show strong evidence. This isn't a coincidence.

07 — The Question

We punish people for outcomes we could have predicted from their birth certificate.

This data doesn't prove that free will doesn't exist. It doesn't resolve the compatibilism debate. What it does is shift the burden of proof.

If the strongest predictors of reoffending are things a person never chose — their neighborhood, their family, their age when the system first got its hands on them — then a justice system that responds primarily with punishment is making a philosophical bet: that individual agency overpowers structural circumstance at the moment of decision.

The data suggests that bet is losing. And we're all paying the cost.

Is that justice — or just habit?
